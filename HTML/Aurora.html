Aurora, Central Luzon

Province of
Aurora

Facing the vast Pacific Ocean, Aurora is a frontier of untamed beauty. It is a land where the Sierra Madre mountains meet the crashing waves, offering a sanctuary of lush rainforests, hidden waterfalls, and a coastal spirit that is both rugged and serene.

As a leading producer of coconut and copra, Aurora's landscape is defined by endless groves that sway with the ocean breeze. Along its 332-kilometer coastline, fisherfolk harvest the riches of the Philippine Sea, sustaining a province deeply rooted in the harmony of land and water.

The Story of Aurora

History of Aurora

From the ancestral lands of the Dumagats to the historic Siege of Baler, Aurora’s journey is one of endurance—a province carved from the Sierra Madre and named after a woman of grace and strength.

01
Origins

The Dumagat & Aeta Heritage

Pre-Colonial Era

Long before it was a province, the wilds of Aurora were home to the Dumagat and Aeta tribes. These indigenous groups lived in harmony with the Sierra Madre, perfecting the art of mountain survival and coastal fishing long before foreign ships touched the Pacific shores.

They remain the spiritual guardians of Aurora’s forests today. The name of the capital, Baler, is believed to have come from the word "balod" (a species of mountain pigeon) or "vale" (a local bird), reflecting the deep connection between the land and its wildlife.

02
Spanish Era

The Famous Siege of Baler

1572 – 1899

In 1609, Franciscan missionaries founded the mission of Baler. The most defining moment of this era was the Siege of Baler (1898-1899), where a small group of Spanish soldiers held out inside the Baler Church for 337 days, unaware that the war had already ended.

In an act of supreme sportsmanship, President Emilio Aguinaldo decreed that the surrendered Spanish soldiers be treated as friends, not prisoners. This event is now celebrated annually as Philippine-Spanish Friendship Day.

03
The Commonwealth

A Tribute to Doña Aurora

1930s – 1951

The province is named in honor of Doña Aurora Aragon Quezon, the beloved wife of President Manuel L. Quezon. Born in Baler, she was known for her humanitarian work and her dedication to the people of the Sierra Madre.

Originally part of Tayabas (now Quezon province), the residents’ desire for independence grew due to the mountain range that isolated them from the rest of the region. In 1951, Aurora was finally recognized as a sub-province thanks to the efforts of local leaders and the Quezon family legacy.

04
Independence

Becoming a Province

August 13, 1979

Through Batas Pambansa Blg. 7, Aurora officially became the 73rd province of the Philippines, separating completely from Quezon. It was also eventually moved from Region 4 to Region 3 (Central Luzon) due to its closer geographical and economic ties to the northern plains.

This independence allowed the province to focus on its own unique potential, transitioning from a remote frontier into a premier destination for eco-tourism and agriculture.

05
The Pacific Frontier

Modern Aurora: Surf & Spirit

1970s – Present

In the late 70s, the film crew of Apocalypse Now left behind surfboards at Sabang Beach, inadvertently sparking the birth of Philippine Surfing. Today, Baler is a world-renowned surf capital.

Beyond the waves, Aurora has flourished as a protected paradise. With the Aurora Pacific Economic Zone (APECO) and a focus on sustainable tourism, the province balances modern progress with the preservation of its vast rainforests and the roaring Pacific coast.

    Experience the Vibrant Festivals of Aurora

    Festivals of Aurora

    Aurora’s festivals are a rhythmic explosion of Pacific culture, gratitude for the harvest, and ancestral pride. The Sabutan Festival in San Luis honors the sturdy native palm through intricate weaving and street dancing. In Baler, the Sheriff’s Festival (also known as the Suman Festival) sees houses draped in thousands of colorful rice cakes as a tribute to San Luis Obispo. Every February, Aurora Day celebrates the province’s founding with the Karatong Festival, echoing the sound of bamboo instruments through the Sierra Madre. These celebrations are the heartbeat of a province that remains wild, free, and deeply connected to its roots.

    AURORAFESTIVAL
    Top 1
    01

    Suman Festival (Baler)

    The Suman Festival is the grandest celebration in Baler, Aurora, held every February 19th. It honors San Luis Obispo and commemorates the birthday of the province's namesake, Doña Aurora Aragon Quezon.

    The town becomes a sea of purple as houses are decorated with thousands of bundles of suman (sticky rice cakes). The "Suman-Draping" is a unique spectacle of gratitude for a bountiful harvest and a celebration of local resilience.

    Top 2
    02

    Sabutan Festival (San Luis - August)

    Celebrated in San Luis, the Sabutan Festival pays tribute to the resilient Sabutan Palm, which has been the primary source of livelihood for local weavers for generations.

    The festival features street dancers adorned in elaborate costumes made from hand-woven Sabutan mats. It showcases the intricate artistry of creating hats, bags, and traditional crafts that define Aurora's artisanal heritage.

    Top 3
    03

    Karatong Festival (Casiguran)

    The Karatong Festival in Casiguran is a rhythmic cultural display centered around the Karatong—a traditional bamboo instrument used as an alarm in ancient times.

    Vibrant contingents dance to the syncopated beats of bamboo, telling stories of how the early settlers used these instruments to warn the community of incoming marauders or to celebrate successful harvests by the Pacific.
