The Story of Bulacan

History of Bulacan

From ancient Tagalog kingdoms to the birthplace of Philippine independence movements, Bulacan's history is woven with patriotism, sacrifice, and cultural brilliance that continues to shape the nation's identity today.

01
Origins

The Wilderness & Early Settlers

Before 1873

Unlike its neighbors, Tarlac was once a vast, densely forested wilderness. It was primarily inhabited by Aeta communities and settled by migrant Pangasinenses and Kapampangan pioneers who cleared the land for agriculture.

These early settlers created a unique cultural blend, turning the "forest" (the possible root of the name 'Tarlac' from the weed Tanglac) into a productive agricultural hub. This diversity is why Tarlac is now known as the Melting Pot of the region.

02
Spanish Colonial

The Birth of the Province

1873

Tarlac was formally established as a military district in 1858 and officially became a province in 1873. It was formed by separating several towns from Pampanga and Pangasinan.

During this era, the Central Azucarera de Tarlac and the development of the Manila-Dagupan Railway turned the province into a vital economic corridor, making it one of the most advanced agricultural centers during the late Spanish period.

03
Revolutionary Era

The Last Capital of the Republic

1898 - 1899

Tarlac played a heroic role in the fight for freedom. It was one of the first eight provinces to rise against Spain.

In 1899, as the American forces advanced, General Emilio Aguinaldo moved the seat of the First Philippine Republic to Tarlac City, making it the provisional revolutionary capital of the Philippines before the government moved further north.

04
World War II

The Sacrifice at Capas

1942 - 1945

Tarlac became the site of one of the darkest chapters of WWII. Camp O'Donnell in Capas served as the final destination of the infamous Bataan Death March.

Thousands of Filipino and American soldiers perished in the concentration camp, a sacrifice now honored by the Capas National Shrine. The province later became a stronghold for the Hukbalahap resistance movement during the Japanese occupation.

05
Modern Development

The Gateway to the North

1945 â€“ Present

Post-war Tarlac transformed into a major industrial and logistics hub. With the opening of the SCTEX and TPLEX, the province has become the primary gateway connecting Manila to Northern Luzon.

Today, the development of New Clark City in Capas is positioning Tarlac as a future-ready metropolis, hosting world-class sports facilities and government hubs, while maintaining its soul as the Culinary and Agricultural heart of the central plains.

    Festivals of Tarlac

    Tarlac's festivals are a grand showcase of its identity as the "Melting Pot of Central Luzon." The Kanlangan Festival in Tarlac City celebrates the province's founding with street dancing and cultural displays that unify its diverse heritage. During the Christmas season, the Belenismo transforms the province into the "Belen Capital of the Philippines," featuring life-sized, artistically crafted nativity scenes. The Iniruban Festival in Camiling honors the harvest of young green rice with music and traditional street performances. These celebrations reflect Tarlac's rich agricultural roots, creative ingenuity, and harmonious community spirit.

    Kanlangan Festival (March)

    The Kanlangan Festival is the grandest celebration in the province, held every March to commemorate the founding anniversary of Tarlac. It serves as a vibrant tribute to the province's identity as the "Melting Pot of Central Luzon," unifying various ethnic groups through culture and arts.

    The festival showcases the "Street Dancing Competition," where participants wear elaborate costumes representing the diverse heritage of Tarlac's municipalities, alongside float parades and trade fairs that highlight local craftsmanship and agricultural abundance.

    Belenismo sa Tarlac (Nov â€“ Jan)

    Belenismo sa Tarlac is a world-class religious and cultural event that has earned the province the title of "Belen Capital of the Philippines." Starting in November, the province is transformed by life-sized, creatively designed nativity scenes (Belen) made from recycled and indigenous materials.

    This competition draws pilgrims and tourists alike to witness the artistic ingenuity of TarlaqueÃ±os. It emphasizes the spiritual core of the Christmas season while promoting environmental sustainability through the use of upcycled decorations.

    Iniruban Festival (Camiling)

    The Iniruban Festival is a unique harvest celebration held every October in Camiling, Tarlac. It honors San Miguel Arcangel and celebrates the local delicacy "Iniruban"â€”a delicious rice cake made from burnt young green rice.

    It features energetic street dancing where performers mimic the traditional process of pounding rice. The festival also hosts the "Chicharon Festival," showcasing Camiling's famous crispy pork delicacies, blending agricultural gratitude with culinary pride.
